# Counted.

## What This Is

Counted exists because millions of Americans oppose the war in Iran and do not know what to do about it.

They are not apathetic. They are not uninformed. They watch the news, they talk to their families, they feel the gas prices, they see the casualty reports. They oppose this war — in historic numbers, more than any war at its inception in modern American history. And they feel completely powerless to stop it.

Counted is an attempt to change that.

## The Problem

The United States is at war. As of late March 2026, the US and Israel have been conducting military operations against Iran for nearly a month. Over 1,500 Iranians have been killed, including children. Thirteen American service members are dead. The conflict has spread to Lebanon, the Gulf states, and beyond. Oil prices have surged. The Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed. There is no congressional authorization. There is no clearly stated endgame. The administration is requesting $200 billion in war funding.

Sixty-two percent of Americans did not want this. Only 38 percent support the strikes — lower than retrospective support for the Iraq War was years after it began. This is the most unpopular a war has ever been when it started.

And yet there is almost no organized pressure on the people who have the power to stop it.

This is not because Americans don't care. It is because the system is designed to make individual opposition invisible. You can call your senator's office. You will be polite. A staffer will note your position. You will hang up and have no idea whether you were the fifth caller or the fifty-thousandth. You will go back to your life feeling exactly as powerless as you did before you picked up the phone.

The senator's office, meanwhile, knows something you don't: they know exactly how many calls they received, and they know that number is small enough to ignore. Not because opposition is small — but because most people who oppose the war never called. They assumed it wouldn't matter. They were probably right, because isolated individual calls rarely do.

This is a collective action problem. The opposition exists. The mechanism to convert it into political pressure does not.

**Counted** is that mechanism.

## What We Believe

**We believe that in a democracy, an overwhelming majority of citizens opposed to a war should be able to stop that war.** That this has not happened is a failure of infrastructure, not of will.

**We believe that constituent pressure works — when it is concentrated, visible, and sustained.** Congressional staffers have said publicly, repeatedly, that high call volume on a single issue changes their boss's calculus. The problem is that "high call volume" requires coordination, and coordination requires tools that ordinary people don't currently have.

**We believe that people are more likely to act when they can see others acting.** The single biggest barrier to participation is the belief that your action doesn't matter. A public, real-time counter of verified constituent contacts — showing that thousands of your neighbors have already called — changes the psychology from "why bother" to "I should be part of this."

**We believe that politicians respond to electoral math, not moral arguments.** Telling a senator that the war is wrong may be true but it is not sufficient. Showing a senator that 48,000 verified constituents in their state have pledged to make the war funding vote their primary consideration in November — that is a number that changes behavior. Counted is designed to produce that number.

**We believe that ordinary Americans should not need to be activists, organizers, or political professionals to make their voice count.** The tool should work for someone with five minutes and a phone. Enter your zip code, see who to call, get a script, make the call, log it, see the counter go up. Done. Five minutes, real impact, visible result.

**We believe in using every legal, transparent, and democratic channel available.** Phone calls, emails, faxes, social media, town halls, office visits, public pledges. All of it. The First Amendment exists precisely so that citizens can petition their government, and we intend to make that right as effective as it can possibly be.

## Who This Is For

Counted is for the parent who sees the casualty reports and feels sick but doesn't know what to do beyond posting on social media.

It is for the veteran who served and now watches the country enter another war without a vote, without a plan, without an exit strategy.

It is for the college student who has never called a congressional office and has no idea how it works or whether it matters.

It is for the small business owner watching gas prices climb toward six dollars and knowing it will get worse.

It is for the person who voted for the current president and feels betrayed by a war they were never asked to support.

It is for the person who didn't vote for the current president and feels helpless to do anything about it.

It is for anyone who has looked at this war and thought: *someone should do something* — and is ready to accept that the someone is them, if only they knew what to do.

Counted tells them what to do.

## What We Are Building

**Phase 1: The Algorithm.** A scoring system that identifies which members of Congress are most likely to be influenced by constituent pressure, based on electoral vulnerability, committee power, demonstrated ambivalence, and a dozen other factors. The output is a ranked list: these are the people to call, in this order, for these specific reasons. Not every senator matters equally. Calling Tom Cotton about Iran is wasted breath. Calling Susan Collins — who chairs the Appropriations Committee, faces reelection in a blue state, and has set public conditions on her support that are already being exceeded — is not.

**Phase 2: The Contact System.** Once you know who to call, the tool makes it effortless. Phone numbers, email addresses, web forms, social media handles, fax numbers, town hall schedules — all in one place, presented in order of effectiveness. Personalized call scripts tailored to each senator's stated positions and vulnerabilities. One-click links to call, tweet, or submit a message. The goal is to reduce the friction between "I want to do something" and "I just did something" to under five minutes.

**Phase 3: The Counted Ledger.** This is the part that changes everything. Every action taken through the system is counted, verified against the user's zip code to confirm they are an actual constituent, and added to a real-time public counter for each senator. The counter is visible to everyone: other constituents, media, organizers, and the senator's office. It shows not just the total but the percentage of the state's electorate it represents, the rate of growth, and the number of people who have pledged to make the war funding vote their deciding factor in November. This counter transforms invisible individual acts into visible collective momentum. It solves the collective action problem by making participation self-reinforcing: each person who acts can see that they are part of something growing, and that growth itself recruits the next person.

## What We Are Not

**We are not partisan.** Counted targets the specific members of Congress — of any party — whose votes on war funding and authorization will determine whether this war continues. The algorithm is indifferent to party affiliation. It cares about persuadability and leverage.

**We are not advocating for any foreign government.** Opposition to the US war in Iran is not support for the Iranian regime. The Iranian government's brutal massacre of its own protesters in January 2026 was an atrocity. Both things are true: the Iranian regime is repressive, and the US military campaign — launched without congressional authorization, with no stated endgame, at a cost of thousands of lives and hundreds of billions of dollars — is wrong. Counted is about what America does with its power, not about defending anyone else's.

**We are not a protest organization.** We are a tool. Protests are valuable and we encourage participation in them. But Counted is specifically designed for the vast majority of Americans who will never attend a protest — and who shouldn't have to in order to make their opposition count.

**We are not breaking any laws.** Every contact method used is public information published by the government itself. Facilitating constituent contact with elected representatives is a constitutionally protected activity. The tool encourages people to contact their own representatives, verified by zip code. No robocalling, no impersonation, no harassment.

## The Stakes

The administration has requested $200 billion in supplemental war funding from Congress. That vote is coming. It is the single most consequential decision point in the near future — more important than any war powers resolution, which has already failed repeatedly along party lines.

The $200 billion request goes through the Senate Appropriations Committee, chaired by Susan Collins, a Republican facing reelection in November in a state that voted for Kamala Harris. This is not an abstraction. This is one person, in one role, with one vote, who can be influenced by constituents from one state.

The question is whether enough people from Maine — and Alaska, and North Carolina, and Texas, and Kansas, and every other state with a persuadable senator — will make their opposition visible, quantified, and impossible to ignore before that vote happens.

That is what Counted is for.

## A Note on Urgency

This is not a long-term civic engagement project. It may become one. But right now, it is an emergency response to an ongoing war that most Americans did not ask for and do not support. The war funding vote could come within weeks. Every day that passes without organized constituent pressure is a day the administration uses to normalize the conflict and build momentum toward the next escalation.

The time to build this is now. The time to use it is now. The people who need it are waiting.
